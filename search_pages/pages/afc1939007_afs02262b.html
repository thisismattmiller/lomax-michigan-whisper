  • Come all you two, one shanty boys with high feet,
  • Where every ye may be,
  • Come shanty and the deacon seat,
  • And listen unto me.
  • I'll sing the song of Gary's rocks,
  • And I hear though you should know,
  • The bravest of all shanty boys,
  • Is our foreman, young Monroe.
  • It began one Sunday's morning,
  • Early daylight did appear,
  • Our logs were piling mountains high,
  • We could not keep them clear.
  • Cheer up, cheer up my river men,
  • Revolve your heart of woe,
  • We'll break the dam on Gary's rocks,
  • Cries our foreman, young Monroe.
  • Some of those boys were willing,
  • While others they hid from sight,
  • For to break a dam on Sunday,
  • They did not think it was right,
  • When six of our Canadian boys
  • Did bother here to go,
  • And break the dam on Gary's rocks,
  • With her tool of young Monroe.
  • They had not picked off many logs,
  • And all to them did say,
  • I must send you back up the drive,
  • My boys, for the dam will soon give away.
  • Alone he freed the keel of bed,
  • And when the dam did go,
  • It carried away on the piling flood,
  • Our foreman, young Monroe.
  • When the rest of the boys up at the camp,
  • The sad news came to hear,
  • In search of his dead body,
  • Down the river they did steer,
  • When there they found to their surprise,
  • Their sorrow, grief and woe,
  • All bruised and mangled on the beach,
  • Laid the corpse of young Monroe.
  • They picked him up most tenderly,
  • Smoothed down his waving hair,
  • There was one perform among them,
  • Whose cry did raise the air,
  • The fairest last of second,
  • All the tears of angry flow,
  • But her mourns and cries,
  • Could not awake her true love,
  • Young Monroe.
  • The missus packed a whip,
  • Only by the riverside,
  • It was her only daughter,
  • Monroe's intended bride.
  • The wait did offer tearless love,
  • The boss to her did pay,
  • And a gift of gold was sent to her,
  • By these shanty boys next day.
  • When she received the money,
  • She thanked them dearly,
  • But it was not her portion,
  • Long on earth would it be,
  • For it was just six weeks or so,
  • When she was called to go,
  • And the shanty boys laid her to rest,
  • By her true love, young Monroe.
  • They buried her quite decent,
  • Like it was at the fourth of May.
  • Come all you true-born shanty boys,
  • And for your comrade pray,
  • Engraved upon a hemlock tree,
  • Whereby the missus grow,
  • Is the name and date,
  • Of the mournful face,
  • Of our shanty boy Monroe.
