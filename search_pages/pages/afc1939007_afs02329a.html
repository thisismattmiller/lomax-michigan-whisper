  • I say, when they built those cars, manufactured those cars in the United States and built those great pavements through the world, through the United States and all the world,
  • it made more hoards than it ever was before in the United States.
  • How did that happen?
  • Well, because they used to have whorehouses them days, you know, here and there and all over, you know, they'd have a whorehouse away from the city.
  • The boys used to go there, see, understand, to the whorehouses, and a decent girl could walk the street anytime and never be insulted.
  • Now they can't, see.
  • And now the whorehouses, they would have barred them all out and take them out of these cars, see.
  • Well, the girls will go out now and they'll stop you, give us a ride, give me a ride, see.
  • The girls nowadays don't want to be insulted, they want to insult you.
  • Years ago, when I remember, now when I was a young fellow, years ago, why, if a girl would stop a man with a horse and buggy on the street and tell him to give her a ride, that would be a dirty insult, you see.
  • Oh, they think that is awful.
  • You see, you know, I'll tell you something, you know.
  • I was in Wisconsin, living in Wisconsin, and I was a young man, you know, and I was fishing there.
  • And my brother had a horse and buggy.
  • I was fishing with him.
  • I went to a farmer's daughter one night, I knew her, Christ, I knew her, what dance was it, and all that.
  • I went there one afternoon, I went in the evening, I went in the evening, I went in the evening, to get her for a buggy ride.
  • And she was willing to go, and she had to ask her father and mother, see.
  • And she asked him, Yes, you can go, but you've got to take Johnny with you.
  • I was a little boy, see.
  • A little boy about ten years old, a little bit older, something like that.
  • He's got to go with you.
  • You think you could get a girl out some days alone in your buggy?
  • Not by a God damn sight.
  • You've got to take Johnny with you, or Jimmy, whoever his name is.
  • Well, that's because it was them days, eh?
  • Let's have that song about Jack now.
  • All right.

  • When, when poor Jack, he came on shore, with his gold and money and stowers,
  • he was led like a horse by the altar,
  • with Bertie Polly by his side, with a dark and a rolling eye, he's contented to the heart like all sailors.
  • The landlady then comes in, she is dressed both knit and trimmed,
  • she appears like some evening star, for to welcome poor Jack in,
  • when she finds him in good trim, chocks him down, for to one at the bar.
  • Then this game was flat carried on, until Jack's money was all gone,
  • not a kind word to be spoken or be heard, with her nasty snarling eye and her nose turned towards the sky,
  • saying it's time you young sailor to be going.
  • Then poor Jack, he makes no reply, but a canter he lets fly.
  • Oh, it's at this old woman in the rage, and the daughters that wear a fright,
