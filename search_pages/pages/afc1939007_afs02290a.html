  • It was once that I was courted by a bonny Irish boy.
  • He called me a jewel, his heart's delight and joy.
  • It was in Dublin City, that place of noted fame,
  • where first my bonny Irish boy, of courting me, came.
  • His cheeks was red as roses, his hair was ill-light brown,
  • his golden locks and ringlets hung o'er his shoulders down,
  • his feet as white as ivory, his eyes as black as flow,
  • and he breaks the heart of all the girls wherever he may go.
  • A long time I kept his company and hoped to be his wife,
  • but now he's gone and left me to cross the region's tide.
  • And I'm afraid some other fair maid my true love would enjoy,
  • while I am left lamenting for my bonny Irish boy.
  • But I'll pack up my clothing and in search of him I'll go.
  • I'll wander for my true love's sake through rain, wind, frost and snow.
  • When I'm sick, sore and sorry, I will sit down and cry,
  • when I think of the joys I had with my bonny Irish boy.
  • When I am dead and gone, there's one request I crave,
  • take my bones to Ireland and lay them in the grave.
  • Those words write on my tombstone to let all pastors by,
  • know that I died quite brokenhearted for my bonny, bonny, bonny.
  • I learned that when I was 17 years of age in the London War.
