  • The month of August, one warm and sultry day, when from the west a gentle gale the wind
  • began to play. A fire was seen a-rising close by the railroad track, on the farm of one
  • little fellows in the town of Shandolack. It started going southward with many a dreadful
  • crash. Then, like a roaring demon, it went thundering through the slash. Oh, such a wild
  • fantastic sight I cannot well describe, as great red tongues of fire flashed from almost
  • every sight. The people of that neighborhood, like heroes brave and true, fought well to
  • protect their homes and help their neighbors too. Of every spark of enmity that might have
  • been before, in such a consternation was thought about no more. But where the fire was raging
  • were those heroes gathered round. There was not one to hang behind, among them to be found.
  • Both great and small were willing to give a helping hand and to keep the fire from burning
  • out the folks in Michigan. A little to the southward of a smoky section line, four settlers
  • were expecting far to have a dreadful time. And one of them, on seeing all the darkness
  • coming o'er, picked up his traps and started with his family to the shore. But the rest
  • of them, like heroes, stayed and temped to fight the fire and struggled to protect their
  • homes before they should retire. Although the fire was raging wild, those heroes did
  • not run, for he who left his property was burned completely down. He had no sonar left
  • his farm, his house was in a flame, and the splendid piece of grain he had, whose fate
  • was just the same. His loss was great, there's no mistake, and shocking it is to see how
  • soon a man may be reduced to want and misery. There was many a field of ripening grain and
  • many a stack of hay were swallowed by those raging flames on that destructive day. All
  • those raging elements have injured many the one. Look over the slash and there you'll
  • see the help that it has done, for now a man can go to work and clear a farm alone and
  • make himself in a few years a comfortable home. It won't be long, here you will see
  • the farmer with his fowl, busy in turning over the soil which is the slashing now, and
  • fields of grain will be harvested with pleasure and contempt, where not a stump will be in
  • sight the reaper to present. 'Twas thus the hand of Providence was working for the best,
  • if not to be felt at present in the future will be blessed, and as I conclude, and all
  • of us have gone to our repose, this dreary slashing will be seen to blossom as the rose.
